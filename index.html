Core Findings

What 25,829 records across 15 years actually demonstrate.
Verdict: Primary school results are statistically meaningless as career predictors

Across 33 of 37 event/age/sex combinations tested over 13 championship years, there is no statistically significant improvement in winning performances (p ≥ 0.05). Year-to-year CV is typically 1–3% — noise, not signal. Only 8.5% of prim champions ever podiumed at Senior. And Kirsti Bell — who placed 13th at age 10 — became Senior Steeplechase Champion seven years later. The data doesn't support early specialisation.

33/37
Events with no significant trend
Over 13 years, 33 of 37 event/age/sex combinations show no statistically significant improvement in winning performances (p ≥ 0.05). The field is neither getting faster nor slower in any meaningful sense.
1–3%
Typical coefficient of variation (CV%)
CV% of annual winning performances hovers at 1–3% for most events — the baseline noise floor of field quality, weather, and measurement variation. This is not a performance trend. It is random variation within a stable system.
8.5%
Hit rate: prim winner to senior podium
Of 3,258 identifiable primary school athletes, only 33 who won a prim event also podiumed at Junior or Senior nationals — 1 in 12. The remaining 91.5% either stopped competing at national level or never made the senior podium.
#13
Kirsti Bell's prim ranking → Senior Champion
Bell placed 13th in the 1200m at age 10 — last in the recorded finalists. She became Senior 3000m Steeplechase Champion in 2019. The single most extreme case in the dataset of early non-selection followed by senior championship success.
176
Career arcs traced across levels
176 athletes matched across Prim and Junior/Senior records. 35 of these — athletes who didn't podium at prim — still podiumed at Junior or Senior level. Career development is not linear and not predictable from age-10 results.
6
Prim medalists who also medaled at Senior
Only 6 athletes in the dataset hold medals at both primary school and Senior national championships. All 6 required 5–7 years of continued development. None converted immediately. Even among the "successful early identifications", patience was the mechanism, not early specialisation.
